Overview

Jacob writes from a pragmatic, systems-level perspective, examining how AI disrupts existing power structures and skill hierarchies. His central argument: resistance to AI often masks deeper fears about professional obsolescence, and the "friction" of working with AI is actually where real learning happens. He consistently challenges the assumption that AI use is inherently easy or passive.

Key Themes

  • Professional obsolescence
  • AI friction as learning
  • Environmental greenwashing
  • Adversarial AI workflows
  • Critical distrust as skill
  • Fluency illusion critique

Argues that professional AI interaction is characterized by friction, not fluency—correcting AI errors requires deep subject knowledge, logic, and rhetorical precision. Challenges the "learned helplessness" argument by reframing AI use as adversarial management.

The Green Mask: What We're Really Hiding Behind January 15, 2026

Exposes environmental objections to AI as a "noble-sounding excuse" masking fears of structural obsolescence. Uses data to show that resistance to AI often comes from defending existing power hierarchies, not genuine ecological concern.

The Obsolescence Trap: Why "Choice" Is Not Enough January 13, 2026

Examines how AI threatens established professional identities and why "choice" rhetoric fails to address structural power shifts. Argues that the real question isn't whether to use AI, but how to maintain relevance in a transformed landscape.
